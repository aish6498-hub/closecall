Every day, space rocks pass closer than the Moon.

Near-Earth objects are asteroids and comets whose orbits bring them within 1.3 AU of the Sun — close enough to warrant tracking. CloseCall pulls live NASA data so you can watch them yourself.

KEY CONCEPTS

Miss Distance

Astronomers measure close approaches in lunar distances (LD) — the average gap between Earth and the Moon: 384,400 km. An asteroid passing at 1 LD is as close as the Moon. At 0.5 LD, it's closer. Most passes on the Feed are between 1–20 LD — still millions of kilometres, but cosmically next door.

PHA Classification

NASA classifies an asteroid as a Potentially Hazardous Asteroid (PHA) if it meets two criteria: its orbit brings it within 0.05 AU of Earth's orbit, and it's at least 140 metres in diameter — large enough to cause regional or greater damage on impact. Size and proximity together trigger the flag. Being a PHA doesn't mean it will hit — it means it's worth watching.

Impact Energy

A 140m asteroid travelling at 20 km/s carries roughly 300 megatons of energy on impact — enough to devastate a major city. Double the size and the energy increases eightfold. This is why NASA's PHA threshold uses both diameter and orbital proximity together — size alone doesn't tell the full story.

THE TORINO SCALE

The Torino Scale is NASA's 0–10 system for communicating the impact threat of a specific asteroid on a specific date. It combines probability of impact with estimated energy released.

VALUE LEVEL MEANING
0 No Hazard Orbit doesn't cross Earth's path, or too small to survive atmosphere
1 Routine Merits monitoring but no cause for public concern
2–4 Merits Attention Close pass with low but non-zero impact probability
5–7 Threatening Significant probability — warrants public attention and preparedness
8–10 Certain Impact Confirmed collision — local destruction (8) to global catastrophe (10)

No known asteroid currently rates above 0. Apophis briefly reached Torino 4 in 2004 before refined calculations dropped it back to 0.

HISTORICAL MOMENTS

1908 Tunguska, Siberia

The Tunguska Event

A rocky asteroid roughly 50 metres across exploded 8–10 km above the Podkamennaya Tunguska River. The airburst released energy equivalent to 3–5 megatons of TNT — flattening an estimated 2,000 km² of forest. No crater, no fragments. Had it arrived 4 hours later, Earth's rotation would have placed it over St. Petersburg.

SIZE ~50 m ENERGY 3–5 MT AREA 2,000 km²
2013 Chelyabinsk, Russia

The Chelyabinsk Airburst

On 15 February 2013, a 20-metre asteroid entered the atmosphere at 19 km/s and exploded 23 km above Chelyabinsk. The shockwave shattered windows across six cities, injuring around 1,500 people — mostly from flying glass. It was not detected in advance. On the same day, asteroid 2012 DA14 made a separate, predicted close pass at 27,700 km. Two independent events, one day.

SIZE ~20 m SPEED 19 km/s INJURED ~1,500
2017 Earth Flyby

3122 Florence

At roughly 4.9 km in diameter, 3122 Florence was the largest asteroid to pass this close to Earth since NASA began tracking near-Earth objects. It passed at 7.1 million km — about 18.5 lunar distances. Florence will not approach Earth this closely again until after 2500.

SIZE 4.9 km MISS DIST 18.5 LD NEXT PASS post-2500
2023 Earth Flyby

2023 BU — Closer Than Satellites

Discovered just four days before its closest approach, 2023 BU passed within 3,600 km of Earth's surface — closer than many geostationary satellites. The car-sized asteroid was never a threat (it would have largely burned up), but the short lead time highlighted the detection gap for small, fast-moving objects. One of the closest flybys ever recorded.

MISS DIST 3,600 km DETECTED 4 days prior SIZE ~4 m
2029 Upcoming

99942 Apophis — The One to Watch

On 13 April 2029, asteroid 99942 Apophis — approximately 375 metres wide — will pass Earth at roughly 31,000 km. Closer than geostationary satellites, and visible to the naked eye across Europe, Africa and western Asia. NASA's OSIRIS-APEX mission will study it during the flyby. When first discovered in 2004, it briefly held a Torino Scale rating of 4. Refined calculations confirmed no impact risk.

SIZE ~375 m MISS DIST 31,000 km DATE 13 Apr 2029
